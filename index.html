Real-Time Marketing Decision Engine

At 10:12, paid search was still buying as if the morning offer held. It did not. Response softened in chat first, then social replies turned colder, then conversion rate slipped hard enough to make the spend curve look irresponsible. The operating problem is not abstract dashboard hunger. It is a live campaign running fifteen to forty minutes ahead of the humans accountable for it. This page describes the room built to close that gap: workshops to pin down decision moments, a governed KPI taxonomy with owners and formulas, near-real-time data pipelines merging feedback, channel performance, and spend, and role-based surfaces that tell each operator what they need to decide before the next media hour starts. Here, “real time” does not mean millisecond theater. It means feedback signals arriving inside two to six minutes, platform and spend facts reconciling inside eight to fifteen, and a staffed workflow that can throttle, swap creative, or reroute budget before waste compounds through the shift.

Shift Prompt 01

Why did performance look acceptable until the wrong hour was already bought?

The control room opens on a failure mode that marketers know but rarely instrument well: the campaign is not exactly blind, but the facts arrive on different clocks and are reviewed by different people. Spend is visible. Clicks are visible. Conversions are visible with caveats. Customer reaction, usually the earliest sign that the message or promise is breaking down, arrives in unstructured fragments and is often treated as a separate service issue instead of a campaign signal. The result is a familiar lag. A growth lead sees the media dashboard still within pacing tolerance and waits. A channel manager sees that one line item is heating up but cannot tell whether it is efficient heat or expensive friction. Support notices tone change first, but the finding sits outside the budget conversation. By the time the views converge, the room is explaining waste instead of preventing it.

This engine is designed around decision moments rather than vanity freshness. Stakeholder workshops define where the room can actually intervene: pause a creative set, throttle a channel, shift budget between cohorts, suppress a deteriorating audience, or hold steady because the signal has not cleared a minimum evidence threshold. That is why the time promises are operationally specific. “Real time” for this room means enough signal, in enough context, soon enough to change the next action. A two-minute chat spike matters because it can force a watch state. A ten-minute spend feed matters because it grounds the cost of waiting. A fifteen-minute alert matters because it is the smallest window the operators trust to separate a genuine performance drift from temporary placement noise.

  • The room treats customer feedback as a campaign signal, not just a service byproduct.
  • Every metric is published with freshness so nobody mistakes a late source for a stable outcome.
  • Decision support is anchored to media hours, shift handoffs, and review cadences, not to abstract streaming claims.
Shift Prompt 02

What can the room trust now, what is still settling, and who needs to know?

Source handling is explicit because a control room collapses without freshness discipline. Feedback sources include surveys, chat transcripts, social replies, CRM notes, and campaign-coded support tickets. They arrive fastest, but they are noisier and need light normalization: campaign mapping, channel tagging, sentiment scoring, issue taxonomy, and deduplication. Channel performance follows from ad platforms and web analytics with their own refresh behavior and attribution caveats. Spend facts may lag more than click facts, and true cost can lag more than visible platform spend when fees, refunds, or offline adjustments are involved. The engine does not flatten these realities. It publishes them.

That design choice sounds plain, but it changes behavior. When the executive view shows that social complaints are fresh to four minutes while platform conversion rate is fresh only to twelve, the room can move into watch mode without pretending the conversion truth is final. When the channel manager sees a missing spend feed flag, they know to avoid over-correcting on one apparently cheap pocket of traffic. When the analyst sees an attribution delay marker, they can frame the scenario view correctly: interim efficiency is a directional read, not the close. This is what makes the system operational rather than theatrical. It acknowledges latency, tells you what is known, and lets action scale with confidence instead of gut feel.

Fast signal

Customer feedback

Chat, surveys, CRM notes, and social replies are the room’s early-warning layer. They should land within two to six minutes, because their job is to flag message breakage before spend-heavy sources finish updating.

  • Used for watch states, issue clustering, and creative confidence checks.
  • Thresholds tolerate moderate noise but punish sustained deterioration.
Core signal

Channel performance

Impressions, clicks, CTR, landing conversion, and cohort splits usually stabilize inside five to twelve minutes. This is the layer that confirms whether the feedback change is also material in behavior.

  • Used for budget shift proposals, audience suppression, and channel throttles.
  • Published with freshness badges and cohort availability markers.
Reconciling signal

Spend and cost

Cost and pacing are allowed eight to fifteen minutes, sometimes longer at source close. They matter because every delayed decision has a spend consequence that should be quantified.

  • Used for pacing interventions, hold-or-cut calls, and finance-safe reporting.
  • Missing spend flags block aggressive reallocation recommendations.

Shift Prompt 03

Which metrics are assembled every five minutes, and which wait for review cadence?

The KPI taxonomy is the backbone. Workshops identify the decision moments; the taxonomy converts those moments into shared formulas, owners, thresholds, and cadences. The room does not just say “efficiency” or “engagement.” It defines a five-minute CPA, a thirty-minute sentiment delta, an hourly budget burn ratio, a daily blended efficiency, and a weekly experiment confidence index. Some metrics are designed for immediate action and use short windows with strong freshness requirements. Others are deliberately slower because they support review, not intervention. That split protects the operators from using a boardroom metric to manage a live shift or using a volatile shift metric to rewrite strategy.

Assembly also standardizes grain. Every signal is aligned by campaign, channel, audience, creative, and time bucket so that role-based views inherit one truth instead of stitching local versions together. Data quality checks run before the metric leaves the pipeline: freshness thresholds, missing-source detection, duplicate event scans, threshold sanity checks, and backtest comparisons against known periods. A metric that fails its guardrail can still be shown, but it is downgraded, badged, and prevented from triggering the stronger interventions. That matters because the control room is not trying to eliminate uncertainty. It is trying to make uncertainty visible and manageable.

Five-minute aggregation loop

with five_minute as (
  select
    campaign_id,
    channel,
    date_trunc('minute', event_time)
      - interval '1 minute' * (extract(minute from event_time)::int % 5)
      as bucket_start,
    sum(spend) as spend,
    sum(clicks) as clicks,
    sum(conversions) as conversions,
    avg(sentiment_score) as sentiment_score
  from marketing_signal_fact
  where event_time >= now() - interval '90 minutes'
  group by 1, 2, 3
)
select *,
  spend / nullif(conversions, 0) as cpa_15m
from five_minute;

Alert threshold policy

alert "campaign_drift" {
  when freshness_minutes <= 15
   and cpa_15m > target_cpa * 1.18
   and sentiment_delta_30m < -0.12
   and spend_feed_complete = true
  then notify ["growth_lead", "channel_manager"]
  action_hint "throttle high-cost cohorts; inspect offer mismatch"
}
Shift Prompt 04

Who needs the same truth, and who needs a different cut of it?

Role-based dashboards are not different truths. They are different decision surfaces over one assembled metric layer. Executives need concentration of risk, major performance shifts, and the cost of waiting. Growth leads need alert context, scenario views, and decisions queued by urgency. Channel managers need drill-downs to audience, creative, and placement. Analysts need cohorts, backtests, and experiment readouts to explain whether a movement is meaningful or temporary. If those views are stitched from different definitions, the room devolves into reconciliation meetings. If they inherit the same taxonomy, governance becomes possible and decisions compress.

The useful detail is role specific. A VP does not need the full issue cluster tree, but they do need to know that negative feedback is concentrated in one region and one offer variation, that the room has twelve minutes before the next pacing checkpoint, and that the recommended intervention is a controlled throttle rather than a hard stop. A channel manager does need the issue cluster tree, because they will decide whether to suppress the audience, swap the creative, or redirect budget into a healthier cohort. The analyst needs a separate view into data quality and historical context because a spike that looks extraordinary on a raw chart may be routine for a particular daypart. The engine supports all of that without asking each role to rebuild the same picture by hand.

Executive

Hourly

Top-line revenue efficiency, budget exposure, risk concentration by campaign, and the cost of waiting. The executive surface is for hold, reroute, or approve intervention calls.

  • Shows only escalated alerts with freshness and financial exposure.
  • Uses scenario cards instead of raw event detail.

Growth Lead

15 min

Pacing, efficiency drift, cohort health, and creative confidence in one board. This is the operating center for interventions during an active campaign shift.

  • Receives threshold alerts with action hints and confidence level.
  • Can compare projected waste from acting now versus waiting one more cycle.

Channel Manager

5 min

Placement, audience, creative, and offer detail with drill-downs into where the degradation begins. This view supports fast tactical moves.

  • Supports creative swaps, audience suppression, and channel throttles.
  • Pairs chart movement with issue clusters from live feedback.

Analyst

Continuous

Quality checks, cohort behavior, backtesting, and A/B readouts. The analyst surface prevents the room from overreacting to artifacts and preserves reproducibility.

  • Flags missing joins, attribution lag, and unstable denominator issues.
  • Feeds governance by documenting why a threshold changed.

Shift Prompt 05

Which decisions does the system accelerate before the next media hour begins?

The close is practical. The system accelerates decisions that save budget, protect message quality, and make post-shift review less political because the evidence trail is intact. It does not promise to automate marketing judgment away. It shortens the path from signal to accountable action. When feedback turns before spend does, the room can watch. When feedback, conversion, and spend all point in the same direction, the room can cut or reroute with confidence. When a test is underpowered or a source is stale, the room can hold, state why, and keep the decision log clean. That is the difference between a dashboard and an operating mechanism.

Backtesting and A/B readouts keep the mechanism honest. Thresholds are not set once and forgotten; they are reviewed against historical shifts, false positives, and campaign seasonality. Governance matters here because KPI drift can be as dangerous as campaign drift. If definitions change without stewardship, last month’s lessons cannot be compared to this month’s controls. So the lifecycle is explicit: propose a KPI change, assess its business meaning, run a backtest, approve the owner, publish the threshold, and record the review cadence. That discipline keeps the room from becoming a collage of clever metrics that no one trusts under pressure.

Budget control

10-15 min

Throttle a deteriorating cohort, cap a channel with rising CPA, or reroute spend toward a healthier segment before the next media hour locks in more waste.

  • Requires spend completeness and stable conversion denominator.
  • Escalates to executive only when exposure exceeds a defined budget threshold.

Message control

5-10 min

Swap creative, suppress an offer variant, or narrow audience targeting when feedback signals show the promise is landing badly before conversion data fully settles.

  • Triggered when feedback deterioration is sustained across more than one source.
  • Logged with issue cluster evidence so postmortems stay concrete.

Experiment control

30-60 min

Continue, stop, or extend A/B tests with enough context to know whether a readout reflects real lift, audience imbalance, or latency distortion.

  • Uses backtesting and cohort parity checks before declaring a winner.
  • Feeds threshold governance when repeated exceptions appear.

Data quality checks

Freshness, duplicate detection, broken joins, cohort completeness, and metric sanity checks run before alert severity is assigned. The room should never page harder because the pipeline is lying.

Backtesting

Historical campaign periods are replayed so operators can tune thresholds to catch costly drift without turning every routine fluctuation into a false alarm.

KPI governance

Formula changes, threshold overrides, and new ownership assignments move through a scheduled review cadence, preserving reproducibility across shifts, launches, and postmortems.

Control-Room Close

The strongest claim this system makes is narrow and useful: it reduces the minutes between market change and accountable marketing action. Not every signal is immediate. Not every metric should be. But the room defines the latency of each source, assembles KPIs on a governed rhythm, routes them into role-specific views, and accelerates the decisions that matter while the shift is still live. That is what “real time” means here: feedback inside minutes, spend close enough to price the wait, analysts guarding quality, and managers changing the next action before the next chunk of budget is gone.

Scope reflected here: stakeholder workshops on decision moments and sources, KPI taxonomy with formulas and owners, near-real-time pipelines merging feedback, channel performance, and spend, role-based dashboards with alerts and scenario views, and governance covering data quality checks, backtesting, A/B readouts, and KPI lifecycle changes.
